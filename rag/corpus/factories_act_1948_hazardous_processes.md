# The Factories Act, 1948 — Chapter IV-A: Provisions Relating to Hazardous Processes

Source: Sections 41A-41H, inserted by the Factories (Amendment) Act, 1987, following
the Bhopal gas disaster (1984). Verified against indiacode.nic.in / advocatekhoj.com
Bare Acts library (https://www.advocatekhoj.com/library/bareacts/factories/index.php).
Quoted clauses below are the operative statutory text; surrounding text is a faithful
paraphrase of the remaining sub-clauses, not verbatim.

## Section 41A — Constitution of Site Appraisal Committees

Every State Government may appoint a Site Appraisal Committee to examine applications
for the establishment of a factory involving a hazardous process. The Committee is
chaired by the Chief Inspector and includes representatives of the state/central
pollution control boards (water and air), the State Environment Department, the
Meteorological Department, an occupational health expert, the Town Planning
Department, and up to five co-opted members. It must examine an application and make
its recommendation to the State Government **within ninety days**. Once the State
Government grants permission on the Committee's recommendation, no further separate
clearance is required from the pollution control boards under the Water (Prevention
and Control of Pollution) Act or the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act.

## Section 41B — Compulsory disclosure of information by the occupier

The occupier of a factory carrying on a hazardous process must:

1. Disclose all information regarding dangers, including health hazards and the
   measures to overcome them, to workers, the Chief Inspector, the local authority,
   and the general public in the vicinity, in the prescribed manner.
2. At the time of registering the factory, lay down a detailed policy on worker
   health and safety, and inform the Chief Inspector and local authority of it (and
   of any subsequent revision).
3. Ensure the disclosed information includes accurate details of the quantity,
   specifications, and characteristics of wastes and their manner of disposal.
4. Draw up an on-site emergency plan and detailed disaster-control measures for the
   factory, and make them known to workers and the local public.
5. Inform the Chief Inspector of the nature and details of the hazardous process
   within 30 days of starting it (or of the 1987 amendment coming into force, if
   already running such a process).
6. Face possible cancellation of the factory licence under Section 6 for failing to
   comply with (5), in addition to other penalties.
7. Establish and widely publicise measures for the handling, usage, transportation,
   and storage of hazardous substances inside the factory, with Chief Inspector
   approval.

## Section 41C — Specific responsibility of the occupier in relation to hazardous processes

Every occupier of a factory involving a hazardous process must:

- **(a)** Maintain accurate, up-to-date health/medical records of workers exposed to
  chemical, toxic, or harmful substances.
- **(b)** Appoint persons with qualifications and experience in handling hazardous
  substances, and who are competent to supervise such handling and provide
  protective measures; the Chief Inspector's determination of "qualified" is final.
- **(c)** Arrange medical examinations of every worker before assignment to a
  hazardous process, during the period of such assignment (at intervals not
  exceeding twelve months), and after cessation of such employment.

## Section 41D — Power of Central Government to appoint an Inquiry Committee

1. Where an extraordinary situation involving a factory engaged in a hazardous
   process has occurred, the Central Government may appoint an Inquiry Committee to
   inquire into the standards of health and safety observed, identify the causes of
   any failure to observe prescribed standards, and recommend measures to prevent a
   recurrence.
2. The Committee has a Chairman and two other members, with terms as the Central
   Government determines.
3. Its recommendations are **advisory in nature** — not binding directives.

## Section 41E — Emergency standards

1. Where the Central Government is satisfied that no standards of safety have been
   prescribed for a hazardous process (or existing ones are inadequate), it may
   direct the Director-General of the Factory Advice Service and Labour Institute,
   or a specialised institution, to lay down **emergency standards**.
2. Emergency standards are enforceable — and have the same effect as if incorporated
   into the rules under the Act — until they are formally incorporated into those
   rules.

## Section 41F — Permissible limits of exposure of chemical and toxic substances

1. The maximum permissible threshold limits of exposure to chemical and toxic
   substances in manufacturing processes (hazardous or otherwise) in any factory are
   as indicated in the Act's **Second Schedule**.
2. The Central Government may amend that Schedule by notification in the Official
   Gazette, based on new scientific evidence from specialised institutions or
   experts.

## Section 41G — Workers' participation in safety management

1. The occupier of every factory with a hazardous process (or where hazardous
   substances are used/handled) must set up a **Safety Committee** with equal
   numbers of worker and management representatives, to promote cooperation on
   safety and health and to periodically review the measures taken. (State
   Governments may exempt specific occupiers by written order.)
2. The composition, tenure, and rights/duties of Safety Committee members are as
   prescribed by rules.

## Section 41H — Right of workers to warn about imminent danger

1. Where workers in a factory engaged in a hazardous process have **reasonable
   apprehension of imminent danger** to life or health from an accident, they may
   bring it to the notice of the occupier/agent/manager/person in charge — directly
   or through their Safety Committee representatives — and simultaneously notify
   the Inspector.
2. The occupier/agent/manager/person in charge must take **immediate action** if
   satisfied the danger exists, and report the action taken to the nearest
   Inspector without delay.
3. If they are **not** satisfied the danger exists, they must still refer the
   matter to the nearest Inspector immediately; the Inspector's decision on whether
   imminent danger exists is **final**.

### Why this chapter is relevant to compound-risk detection

Chapter IV-A exists precisely because individually-compliant conditions in a
hazardous-process factory can combine into an emergency no single rule anticipated —
which is why it leans so heavily on disclosure (41B), competent supervision (41C),
worker-side escalation (41H), and committee-based review (41A, 41G) rather than a
single numeric threshold. Section 41H in particular is the direct statutory basis for
treating a worker's or a system's "this combination looks wrong" signal as
actionable, even before it crosses any single published limit (41F).
